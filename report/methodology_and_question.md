The methodology selected for this study is Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA), a social scientific technique described by Ragin in 1987 that identifies necessary and sufficient conditions for a desired outcome: in this case, success of recovery after a civil war. This outcome is measured using the Correlates of War project's database; recovery was considered failed when one cessation of hostilities (EndYear) was followed by an additional start of conflict (StartYear), and successful when there was no following StartYr. Footnote: the end date was "the last major engagement after which fatalities declined below the war fatality threshold" (COW Codebook, p. 7, https://correlatesofwar.org/wp-content/uploads/Intra-StateWars_Codebook.pdf). QCA was performed on a number of factors identified by theoretical research as relevant to this outcome, including economic improvement measured as change in GDP per capita, increase in participatory democracy, fuel exports, and others. Though previous studies have analyzed the success of peacekeeping operations after civil wars with a longer time frame than the present one (Olsen, 2024), this study's narrow scope is focused on discerning differences between internationalized and non-internationalized civil wars by performing QCA on two separate datasets distinguished by this difference with a smaller temporal scope due to changes in statecraft in recent decades. Therefore, the use of QCA is predominantly explanatory and designed to contribute to and support theoretical research on civil war recovery by testing existing theory (on what is influential for recovery) and potentially extending an important theoretical distinction to new grounds (on whether the internationalization of civil wars impacts what is relevant to recovery).

Qualitative Comparative Analysis is heavily based on mathematical set theory; it considers whether or not a particular case is included or excluded from a set (for example, whether or not a UN peacekeeping mission was present), and being assigned a 1 on that condition if it is included and a 0 if excluded. I will be performing fuzzy-set QCA, which is based on fuzzy inclusion-exclusion parameters. This requires "calibration", which determines whether or not a certain case is fully included (1), fully excluded (0), at the crossover point (0.5), or somewhere in between. Calibration requires determining what qualifies as inclusion, exclusion, and crossover, where the crossover point can be especially important for being the "point of maximum ambiguity" (Ragin, 2000). This calibration is important in translating the quantitative layer of raw data into the qualitative layer of conditions: one useful example I've seen is that when water goes from 1C to -1C, this quantitative shift encompasses a qualitative change (freezing), but the same quantitative shift from -1C to -3C causes no further qualitative change, since it remains frozen (Mello, 2021, p. 145). Though I have seen debate on how to go about calibration, I will be calibrating primarily based on prior theoretical research rather than descriptive statistics, with slight changes to get closer to the desired logistic shape to have a decent number of cases spread across the range of 0-1.

QCA is partially limited by the few number of conditions that can be effectively used with it: while methodologies like linear regression are capable of handling vast numbers of potential regressors related to the studied outcome, the use of truth tables for QCA means that, given $k$ conditions, the number of rows in the truth table is $2^k$ (since there must be one row for each combination of boolean true/false conditions). These combinations are important since a combination of conditions may be important (for example, with substantial participation in governance from all sectors of society, control of fuel and therefore benefitting from fuel income, may be less contentious; i.e., fuel = 1 AND participation = 1 => recurrence = 0, but fuel = 1 AND participation = 0 => recurrence = 1). Given the few number of cases QCA is designed to study, this means that, given datasets like the ones used here with 30-50 different cases, only 5-6 conditions should be analyzed. The scope of this study is therefore vastly different from other methodologies. It does not seek to provide all, or even a significant amount, of the explanatory factors for the success or failure of civil war recovery: instead, it quantitatively evaluates the relevance of the distinction between internationalized and non-internationalized civil wars to be elucidated by further study. (much fo this is from Mello, p. 64) In light of this, "QCA studies should always provide a careful *justification* for their selection of conditions" (Mello, p. 66).

The analysis itself involves determining necessity of the conditions, defined as whenever the outcome occurs, those conditions are present (in set-theoretic language, the set of cases with the desired outcome are a subset of the set of cases in which those conditions are present). This takes the truth table of $2^k$ rows based on the combination of factors (this is more complicated for fuzzy-set QCA, but is not worth getting into the weeds of for the time being). This truth table is then analyzed by QCA software (though it can be done by-hand with fewer cases) to determine the confluences of factors that lead to the desired outcome and removing the factors whose presence or absence does not affect the outcome. The statistics generated in this analysis are "inclusion/consistency", "coverage", and "relevance of necessity". Consistency measures how much of the set of cases with the desired outcome is in actuality a subset of the set of the cases in which that condition is present; this provides insight into the strength of the relation, where a consistency of 0.9 is the recommended lowest level to find a strong set relation of necessity between the outcome set and condition set. Coverage measures how trivial a given condition is based on how frequently it appears overall; if a condition covers all cases, then though the outcome set is a subset of the condition set, so is the complement of the outcome set. Therefore, though it would be considered highly necessary by the consistency measure, practically, it is irrelevant. A common third metric developed later is "relevance of necessity" that measures how close the condition varible tends to be near-constant, which measures triviality in a way not caught by coverage. Generally, these last two measures should both be above 0.5 to indicate non-trivial conditions.
